  • Capuzzo, Guy The Complement Union Property in the Music of Elliott Carter
    • I

      CUP (Complement Union Property): the union of any member of sc
      X with any non-intersecting member of sc Y is always
      a member of sc Z, where Set class consistency is not guaranteed
      when one combines any member of one set class with each non-intersecting
      member of a second set class.

      II

      Definition I: The term X ∪ Y CUP with scs X and Y if for all
      non-intersecting pcsets x ∈ sc X and y ∈ sc Y. x ∪ y is always
      a member of sc Z

      Definition II: The term X ∪ Y CUP partition of sc Z denotes
      two scs X and Y with which sc Z has CUP

      Definition III: A realization of an X ∪ Y CUP partition of
      sc Z consists of the union of any non-intersecting pcsets x ∈ sc X
      and y ∈ sc Y

      Definition IV: If S is a set and G is a group
      An action of G on S assigns to each g in G a transformation on a set S.
      Which maps any element s of S to an element denoted (s)(g). These
      transformations describe how the elements of the group G act on the
      set S

      Definition V: Suppose the group G acts on the set S, and let s be an element of S
      The orbit of s is the subset of S consisting of all elements of the form
      (s)(g) as g ranges through the elements of the group G

      Definition VI: Given an orbit J containing n elements
      an orbit subset of J is a subset of J that contains x elements, where
      0 < x < n

    • Contextual Inversion

  • Straus, Joseph N. Contextual-Inversion Spaces Journal of Music Theory
    • Abstract This article creates transformational spaces for interpreting
      progressions of sets belonging to the same T/I equivalence class. Within these
      spaces, sets are placed in proximity based upon contextual inversion,
      specifically those contextual inversions that (like the familiar neo-Riemannian
      L, P, and R) preserve common tones. Sequential enchaining of contextual inversions
      will generally define simple, straight-line motions within the spaces described
      in this article. The musical motions of works by Webern, Schoenberg, and Stravinsky
      are measured against the systemicstandard enshrined in the spaces, with both
      conformity and deviation suggesting interpretive possibilities.

      Introduction:

      Compositional spaces (transformational space) are created via
      interpreting progressions of sets belonging to the same T/I equivalence
      class. Sets are placed in proximity based on contextual inversion. Contextual
      inversion spaces provide a spatial representation that places each set
      in close proximity to sets related by inversion around shared tones and
      permit interpretation of harmonic motions among inversionally equivalent sets
      in embodied terms.

      The purpose of this paper is and extension of David Lewin's idea of
      contextual inversion. Lewin's interest in a transformation in which one
      or more notes of a series or collection are fixed and the remainin notes invert
      around them.This common tone preserving inversion idea falls in line with neo-
      Riemannian theory, especially with the concept of RI-chaining.

      Trichordal RI-Chains:

      Ri-Chaining (RICH) trichords superimpose the prime and inverted (and transposed) form
      such that the last two notes of the first series (prime form) become the first two notes
      of the second (inverted). In total, there are three distinct orderings for any trichord:
      x-y-z, x-z-y, and y-x-z.

      Combination Cycle

      Combination Cycles, from Lambert (1990), refers to the individual pitch classes within
      each series and especially the ordered pitch-class intervals between them. (With this, trichordal
      RI-chains can be thought of as segments (called Combination cycles))

      RI-chains and combination cycles offer two complementary perspectives on the same musical
      phenomenon, with RI-chains focusing attention on the serial pitch relations and combination
      cycles on the corresponding interval relations. Every combination cycle entails a trichordal
      RI-chain, and every RI-chain entails a combination cycle of directed pc-intervals.

      Neo-Riemannian LPR-relations and Tonnetze for all twelve trichord classes

      For any trichord, there are three contextual inversions that map any two of its pc onto each
      other. (ideas from Richard Cohn's Audacious Euphony LPR-relations, formulas are shown in
      equations section)
